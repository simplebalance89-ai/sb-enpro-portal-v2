## 1. FILTRATION FUNDAMENTALS

### What is Filtration?

Separation of particles from a fluid (liquid or gas) using a porous medium. The filter medium captures contaminants while allowing the clean fluid to pass through.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Micron (um)** | Unit of measurement. 1 micron = 0.001 mm. Human hair is approximately 70 um. |
| **Absolute Rating** | 99.9%+ removal of particles at rated size. Validated, reproducible performance. Beta ratio >= 5000 (99.98% removal efficiency). Required for pharmaceutical, semiconductor, catalyst protection. |
| **Nominal Rating** | 60-98% removal of particles at rated size (varies by manufacturer). Non-validated, variable performance. Adequate for coarse prefiltration only. |
| **Flow Rate** | Volume per time (GPM, LPM, m3/hr) |
| **Differential Pressure (Delta P)** | Pressure drop across the filter. Clean Delta P: 0.5-2 psi typical. Changeout Delta P: 15-35 psid depending on application. Never exceed manufacturer max rating (collapse risk). |
| **Dirt Holding Capacity** | Amount of contaminant a filter can hold before requiring replacement |

### Particle Retention Mechanisms

1. **Sieving** -- Particles larger than pore size are physically trapped on the filter surface
2. **Depth Filtration** -- Particles trapped within the media matrix throughout its thickness
3. **Adsorption** -- Particles attracted to the media surface by electrostatic or charge-based forces (Zeta potential)
4. **Inertial Impaction** -- Particles deviate from the fluid flow path and impact into the media fibers

### Beta Ratios (Filtration Efficiency)

| Beta Ratio | Efficiency | Rating Classification |
|------------|------------|----------------------|
| Beta = 2 | 50% | Nominal |
| Beta = 10 | 90% | Nominal |
| Beta = 75 | 98.7% | Nominal |
| Beta = 200 | 99.5% | Absolute |
| Beta = 1000 | 99.9% | Absolute |
| Beta = 5000 | 99.98% | Absolute |

**Formula:** Efficiency = (Beta - 1) / Beta x 100%

**Beta 5000 Definition:** Modified ISO 4572 test; 99.98% removal efficiency at stated micron rating. This is the standard for critical contamination control applications.

### Filter Types by Mechanism

| Type | How It Works | Best For |
|------|--------------|----------|
| **Depth** | Particles trapped throughout media thickness via sieving plus adsorption | Pre-filtration, high dirt loads, deformable particles |
| **Surface / Membrane** | Particles captured on the surface; tightest pore structure | Final filtration, sterile applications, colloids |
| **Pleated** | Folded media increases surface area significantly | Balance of capacity and efficiency; rigid particles |
| **Bag** | Large surface area, high flow capacity | Bulk contamination removal, low cost per gallon |

### Nominal vs Absolute Ratings Explained

**Nominal Rating:**
- Retention of approximately 85-95% at rated particle size (some manufacturers claim 60-98%)
- Specifies equivalent diameter of smallest particle with 90% retention
- Varies widely by manufacturer -- not standardized
- Good for: Pre-filtration, general process, non-critical applications
- Cannot maintain consistent low contamination levels

**Absolute Rating:**
- Retention of 99.9%+ at rated particle size
- Validated, reproducible performance under controlled test conditions
- Industry standard: Beta 5000 (99.98% removal) or Log 6 reduction (99.9999%)
- Required for: Sterile filtration, critical hydraulics, catalyst protection, pharmaceutical

### Filtration Variables Impact Table

| Variable | Effect on Efficiency | Effect on Filter Life |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Flow Rate** | Decrease flow = increase efficiency | Decrease flow = increase life |
| **Differential Pressure (PSID)** | Decrease Delta P = increase efficiency | Decrease Delta P = increase life |
| **Viscosity** | Decrease viscosity = increase efficiency | Decrease viscosity = increase life |
| **Contaminant Type** | Depends on pore/particle relationship | Nondeformable particles promote filter cake |
| **Flow Conditions** | Pulsing/surging is detrimental | Steady flow = longer life |
| **Chemical Compatibility** | Incompatibility threatens efficiency | Incompatibility threatens life |
| **Filter Area** | Increase area = increase efficiency | Increase area = longer life |

### Depth vs Surface Filter Comparison

| Parameter | Surface Filters (Pleated) | Depth Filters |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------------|
| **Deformable Particles** | May blind off pleats | Recommended -- adsorptive retention throughout media |
| **Nondeformable Particles** | Removes narrow range; builds cake on surface | Removes broader range throughout matrix |
| **Rating** | Absolute or nominal | Absolute or nominal |
| **Primary Purpose** | Classification (sharp size cutoff) | Clarification (broad range removal) |
| **Flow per 10"** | Recommended 10 GPM | Recommended 5 GPM |
| **Economics < 10 microns** | Holds more dirt, higher flow rate | More economical at > 10 microns |
| **Cartridge Cost** | More expensive per cartridge, fewer replacements | Less expensive per cartridge, more frequent replacement |
| **Housing Cost** | Fewer cartridges = smaller housing | More cartridges = larger housing |

### Particle Type to Filter Selection

| Particle Type | Recommended Filter | Why |
|---------------|-------------------|-----|
| Rigid particles | Pleated surface filter | Will not deform; builds efficient cake on surface |
| Deformable particles | Depth filter | Adsorptive retention throughout media prevents breakthrough |
| Colloids / in suspension | Membrane filter | Requires tightest pore structure for reliable capture |

```
Number of cartridges = System Flow (GPM) / Flow per cartridge (GPM)
```

### Pressure Drop Considerations

- **Clean Delta P:** 0.5-2 psi typical for new filter
- **Changeout Delta P:** 15-35 psid depending on application
- **Max Delta P:** Never exceed manufacturer rating (collapse risk)

### Pressure Escalation Triggers

- **Standard housings:** Most rated to 150 PSI maximum
- **Over 150 PSI:** Requires SSC family housings or equivalent high-pressure design. Escalate to EnPro engineering for review.
- **Over 300 PSI:** Specialty vessels required. Always escalate for engineering review.
- **Sour service (H2S):** NACE MR0175 materials required regardless of pressure.

### Constraint Hierarchy (Safety First)

When multiple constraints apply, prioritize IN THIS ORDER:

1. **SAFETY** -- NACE materials for sour service, pressure ratings, temperature limits. NEVER compromise safety for cost.
2. **CERTIFICATION** -- FDA, 3-A, NSF 61, ASME, USP Class VI. Required certifications are non-negotiable.
3. **PERFORMANCE** -- Micron rating, efficiency (absolute vs nominal), flow rate, dirt holding capacity.
4. **COST** -- Only optimize for cost AFTER safety, certification, and performance requirements are met.

**CRITICAL:** Never recommend the cheapest option if it compromises safety or certification requirements.

1. **Abrasive** -- Hard particles cut and scratch surfaces

4. **Fatigue** -- Cyclic stress creates cracks and spalling
5. **Corrosive** -- Chemical attack accelerated by mechanical wear

### Filter Sterilization Procedures

#### Steam-in-Place (SIP)
- **Temperature:** 121C minimum
- **Pressure:** 15 psig minimum
- **Duration:** 30 minutes
- **CRITICAL:** Maximum 5 psid differential pressure across filter during steaming
- **Safety:** No superheated steam; slow venting required

#### Autoclave
- **Standard:** 121C for 60 minutes OR 130C for 45 minutes
- **Max differential:** 5 psid
- **Application:** Off-line sterilization of filter cartridges

#### Vacuum Autoclave
- **Options:** 121C/30min, 126C/20min, OR 134C/15min
- **Equipment:** Requires vacuum-capable autoclave
- **Advantage:** Faster cycle times at higher temperatures

#### Sealkleen
- **Method:** Hot water sanitization
- **Temperature:** 85-95C
- **Duration:** 30+ minutes
- **Application:** Cold-sensitive materials or routine sanitization

#### Ethylene Oxide (ETO)
- **Temperature:** 45-60C
- **Gas concentration:** 450-1200 mg/L
- **Humidity:** 40-80% RH
- **Duration:** 2-24 hours depending on load
- **Aeration:** 8-12 hours minimum post-sterilization
- **Application:** Heat-sensitive materials only

## 6. OIL & GAS APPLICATIONS

### 1. Acid Gas Sweetening (Amine Systems)

**Process:** Remove H2S and CO2 from sour natural gas using amines (typically MDEA, aMDEA, or MEA). The amine solution absorbs acid gases in a contactor tower and is regenerated in a stripper for reuse.

**ROOT CAUSE of Amine Foaming:** Hydrocarbon contamination in the amine solution. Liquid hydrocarbons entering the amine loop create stable foam in the contactor. The root cause is HC in amine — not the amine itself. Solution: Liquid-liquid (L/L) coalescer (Pall LLS Series, PhaseSep) to remove hydrocarbons from rich amine before they recirculate.

**Multi-Point Filtration Approach (Amine System):**
1. **Contactor Inlet** — L/G coalescer (SepraSol Plus) removes aerosol from inlet gas
2. **Rich Amine** — L/L coalescer (PhaseSep, LLS Series) removes HC contamination (root cause of foaming)
3. **Lean Amine Loop** — Particulate filter (Ultipleat HF) maintains amine clarity <5 ppmw
4. **Contactor Outlet** — L/G coalescer recovers amine carryover from sweet gas

**Operating Challenges:**
- Foaming from hydrocarbon and particulate contamination (ROOT CAUSE = HC in amine)
- Reduced production capacity due to poor gas-liquid contact
- Amine mechanical losses (carryover in dry gas)
- Dirty amine (discoloration = fine corrosion products): clear or pale yellow is normal; dark brown/green/black indicates contamination

**Filtration Solutions:**

| Location | Product | Performance | Benefit |
|----------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Contactor Inlet** (wet gas) | SepraSol Plus, Medallion HP | 99.999% @ 0.3um, 1 ppb downstream | Prevents foaming, protects amine loop |
| **Lean Amine Loop** | Ultipleat High Flow, Nexis, Profile II | Target <5 ppmw solids | Reduces foaming, keeps amine clear |
| **Contactor Outlet** (sweet gas) | SepraSol Plus (B grade media) | 99.999% @ 0.3um | Amine recovery, prevents carryover |
| **Rich Amine** (before regenerator) | PhaseSep L/L coalescer | Hydrocarbon removal | Protects sulfur plant downstream |

**Key Spec:** Amine should be clear or pale yellow; dark brown/green/black indicates contamination requiring filtration intervention.

### 2. Adsorbent Dehydration (Molecular Sieve)

**Process:** Remove water from natural gas to prevent hydrates, corrosion, and catalyst poisoning downstream. Molecular sieve beds adsorb water and are regenerated with hot gas.

**Operating Challenges:**
- Desiccant bed degradation (normal life: 3-5 years if protected)
- Free water causes bed fracturing and dusting
- Hydrocarbon contamination burns onto granules during regeneration
- Desiccant fines damage downstream compressors and equipment

**Filtration Solutions:**

| Location | Product | Performance | Benefit |
|----------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Bed Inlet** | SepraSol Plus, Medallion HP | 99.999% @ 0.3um, dual sump for water slugs | Extends desiccant life, prevents bed fouling |
| **Bed Outlet** (dry gas) | DGF, MCC 1401, Profile Coreless | Particulate removal | Removes desiccant fines, protects downstream |

**Critical:** Dual sump feature accepts water slugs without bypass; oleophobic treatment allows quick recovery after liquid upset events.

### 3. Glycol Dehydration

**Process:** Use glycol solvents (TEG, DEG) to remove water from wet natural gas. Lean glycol absorbs water in a contactor and is regenerated in a reboiler.

**Multi-Point Filtration Approach (Glycol System):**
1. **Contactor Inlet** — L/G coalescer (SepraSol Plus) removes liquid aerosols from wet gas before contactor
2. **Lean Glycol Loop** — Particulate filter (Ultipleat HF, Marksman) maintains glycol clarity <5 ppmw
3. **Rich Glycol** — Activated carbon filter for hydrocarbon/BTEX removal before reboiler
4. **Contactor Outlet** — L/G coalescer recovers glycol carryover from dry gas

**Operating Challenges:**
- Foaming from hydrocarbon and particulate ingression
- Glycol carryover in dry gas (mechanical losses increase operating costs)
- Dirty glycol (brown color = contamination)
- High water content in sales gas (off-spec)

**Filtration Solutions:**

| Location | Product | Performance | Benefit |

### 4. Whisky Depth Filtration

**Application:** Polishing and sterilizing whisky before bottling using depth filter sheets.

**Theory: Zeta Potential**

Depth filters retain particles by screening and **adsorptive forces**. Zeta potential is the critical mechanism:
- Filter surface has positive/negative charge
- Creates electrical double layer with oppositely charged ions
- Shear plane (mono-molecular layer) vs mobile layer
- Equilibrium between flow potential and Zeta potential determines performance

**Critical Factors:**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Temperature** | Higher temp reduces adsorptive forces; lower temp encourages agglomeration |
| **Flow rate** | Higher flow increases Zeta potential; reduces physical retention of oily droplets |
| **Pressure pulsations** | "Breathing" effect allows particles to migrate through matrix |
| **Pump type** | Non-pulsating pumps essential; pulsating pumps change Zeta potential |

**Seitz-K Depth Filter Specifications:**

## 12. TROUBLESHOOTING

### Short Filter Life

1. Check upstream contamination levels -- may be higher than expected
2. Verify filter rating matches application -- too fine captures too much
3. Consider adding prefiltration to protect the final filter
4. Check for emulsions or gels that blind the media
5. Verify chemical compatibility (degradation shortens life)

### High Pressure Drop

1. Undersized filter -- increase surface area (more or larger cartridges)
2. Filter plugged -- replace and investigate contamination source
3. Viscosity change -- check fluid temperature (cold fluid = higher viscosity = higher Delta P)
4. Wrong media -- verify chemical compatibility (swelling, softening)

### Poor Filtration (Particles Passing Through)

1. Filter bypass -- check seals, O-rings, and installation
2. Wrong micron rating -- use a finer filter
3. Particle type mismatch -- try different media (depth vs surface)
4. Channeling -- replace degraded filter (chemical attack, age)
5. Nominal filter in critical service -- upgrade to absolute rated

### Key Specifications to Gather for Any Application

1. **Fluid:** Type, viscosity, temperature, chemical composition
2. **Flow Rate:** Normal operating, peak demand
3. **Pressure:** Operating, max allowable Delta P
4. **Particles:** Type, size distribution, concentration (ppm or mg/L)
5. **Cleanliness Target:** Micron rating, ISO code, sterile requirement
6. **Environment:** Indoor/outdoor, hazardous area classification, sanitary requirement
7. **Changeout:** Frequency preference, maintenance access constraints
